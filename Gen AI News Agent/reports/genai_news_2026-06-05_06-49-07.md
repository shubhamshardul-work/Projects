## 🔬 Research Frontiers

### **[TailLoR: Protecting Principal Components in Parameter-Efficient Continual Learning](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06494v1)**
This paper introduces TailLoR, a novel method for parameter-efficient continual learning that safeguards principal components during spectral decomposition-based finetuning. Researchers in lifelong learning and model adaptation will find this work crucial for developing more robust and efficient AI systems that can learn new tasks without forgetting old ones.

### **[HANDOFF: Humanoid Agentic Task-Space Whole-Body Control via Distilled Complementary Teachers](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06493v1)**
HANDOFF proposes a new approach for humanoid robot control, focusing on agentic task-space whole-body control through distilled complementary teachers. This research is vital for advancing the deployment of humanoid robots in real-world scenarios, offering a more intuitive and robust interface between high-level task planning and low-level motor control.

### **[Code2LoRA: Hypernetwork-Generated Adapters for Code Language Models under Software Evolution](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06492v1)**
Code2LoRA presents a method using hypernetwork-generated adapters to provide repository-level context to code language models, addressing challenges in software evolution. This work offers a promising direction for improving the contextual understanding and adaptability of code LLMs, making them more effective for real-world development environments.

### **[TempoVLA: Learning Speed-Controllable Vision-Language-Action Policies](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06491v1)**
TempoVLA introduces a new paradigm for robot manipulation, enabling learning of speed-controllable Vision-Language-Action (VLA) policies that can adapt execution speed based on task risk. This research is critical for developing safer and more efficient robotic systems capable of nuanced interaction in dynamic environments.

### **[Pretraining Recurrent Networks without Recurrence](http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06479v1)**
This paper explores a novel approach to pretraining recurrent neural networks (RNNs) without relying on traditional recurrence, addressing the challenges of credit assignment across long sequences. This foundational research could lead to more efficient and scalable training methods for sequential data, impacting areas from natural language processing to time series analysis.
